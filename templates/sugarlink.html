Sugar Link

There has been a growing trend of sugar-sweetened beverages that entertain our taste buds while leaving our health much less thrilled.

Let us explore the potential association between the consumption of those beverages and fluctuations in blood glucose levels, a crucial aspect of metabolic health.

Blood glucose level is a key indicator of diabetes.

Condition Blood sugar before meal (mg/dL) Blood sugar after meal (mg/dL)
Diabetes >= 126 >= 200
Prediabetes 110 to 125 140 to 199
Normal <110 <140

Increase in sugar intake can increase the risk of diabetes by leading to obesity and other health conditions.

Our study aims to answer simple questions: Does drinking certain beverages have an effect on blood sugar levels? Is there a higher impact of these beverages over other factors such as neck circumference and waist circumference on blood sugar levels?

The beverages considered include:

Fruit juices

Aerated drinks and energy drinks.

About the Dataset and Method:

The dataset utilized in this analysis comprises the population from Ahmedabad University, spanning characteristics such as gender, waist circumference, neck circumference and beverage consumed. By employing advanced statistical methods, we aim to discern patterns and trends within the data to highlight the possible relationship between consumption of some beverages and blood glucose levels.

There were two methods employed to try to find the correlation between these variables.

  • First we used ANOVA (for a single factor) to test the hypothesis that consumption of beverages has a visible impact on blood sugar. Analysis of Variance is used to determine if there is a statistically significant difference between the means of two or more groups of values.
  • Second, we used the Pearson correlation to find correlation values between glucose levels, waist circumference, neck circumference and consumption of beverages. The Pearson correlation coefficient is a descriptive statistic, meaning that it summarizes the characteristics of a dataset. Specifically, it describes the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two quantitative variables.

Key Findings:

Result of ANOVA:  No observable variation was found in the mean blood glucose levels of people who consumed sugar sweetened beverages like fruit juices and energy drinks and people who did not consume it.

Result of Correlational Analysis:  It shows that apart from the negative correlation between glucose and sugar consumption, rest of the variables are positively related. The highest correlation out of all the variables is found between waist circumference and glucose levels. But we cannot make concrete conclusions based on these results because the correlational values are not significantly huge to draw conclusions from.

Although the statistical analysis was not able to point out anything, many research studies have implied that consumption of sweetened beverages increases the sugar levels and can indirectly be one of the reasons leading to diabetes or exacerbate it.

Limitations of the Study:

It is essential to note that the blood glucose levels recorded in the health fair dataset are random blood glucose levels, with fasting (before meal) or postprandial (after meal) glucose levels left unspecified. Because of that, the results of the associations between beverage consumption and blood glucose levels and conclusions that we are trying to draw based on this dataset might not be fully accurate.
