Age

The risk of diabetes and prediabetes can increase significantly as we age. The elderly population is more likely to have diabetes and prediabetes in comparison to younger individuals. This condition can cause complications in eyes, the heart and even kidneys.

The higher prevalence of diabetes can be directly related to the aging process or indirectly related to age related risk factors such as central obesity and inflammation. Our bodies become less sensitive to insulin as we grow older. This can lead to glucose intolerance which is a situation in which our cells find it challenging to use glucose for energy. Moreover, beta cells of the pancreas may not even function properly. Hence, there are two effects that happen: 1) reduced insulin sensitivity or elevated insulin resistance; and 2) impaired beta cell activity in the pancreas.

Gender

Men and women are affected differently by type 2 diabetes. The complex interactions between certain hormones highlight the different effects that diabetes has on men and women. It is dependent on the reproductive phase as well. It may also differ according to age and weight.

There are more diabetic men before puberty, while there are more diabetic women after the age of menopause and in old age. Women are more likely to have impaired glucose tolerance, which causes problems for the body in metabolizing sugar from diet. Women who have Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) or go through early menopause, as well as men with low testosterone levels, are more likely to acquire type 2 diabetes. Women may have higher risk due to more complex hormonal changes. Furthermore, in many circumstances, women are less likely to be diagnosed and receive treatment due to existing gender inequalities and discrimination.

BMI

Body Mass Index can help to measure an individualâ€™s body fat. An increase in BMI increases the risk of diabetes as well as prediabetes. A rise in BMI is positively associated with the blood glucose levels and diabetes.

Excess weight and obesity are considered to be a major contributing factor to type 2 diabetes and its complications for both men and women. The risk of both prediabetes and diabetes is higher amongst the overweight or obese individuals as compared to the non-overweight individuals.

However, weight gain may increase the risk of diabetes regardless of the individual being overweight or not. The level of risk varies with the weight of an individual.

  • Prediabetes: A condition in which blood sugar levels are higher than normal but not high enough to be classified as type 2 diabetes.
  • Central obesity: Accumulation of excess fat in the abdominal area
  • Insulin: A hormone that regulates the level of glucose in the blood.
  • Beta cells: Cells in the pancreas which produce insulin.
  • Insulin resistance: This condition occurs when the cells of the body do not respond properly to insulin.
  • PCOS: Polycystic ovary syndrome is characterized by the formation of small fluid filled cavities (cysts) in the ovaries. Ovaries produce an abnormal amount of male sex hormones in this condition.
  • Testosterone: A hormone required for the development and maintenance of male characteristics including deep voice, muscle growth and facial hair.
  • BMI = weight (in kg) / height2 (in m2)
